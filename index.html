About Me

I am an NSERC-funded postdoctoral researcher in the Department of Psychology at the University of Toronto, working with Dr. Keisuke Fukuda.

Earlier, I completed a postdoctoral fellowship at Western University with Dr. Ingrid Johnsrude. I received my PhD in Psychology (Cognitive Neuroscience) from the University of Toronto in 2023, working with Dr. Morris Moscovitch and Dr. Claude Alain. My academic training began at McGill University, where I completed an Honours BSc in 2018 under the supervision of Dr. Stephen McAdams.

A question that excites me

Sensory input alone rarely provides a complete picture of the world. How does the brain use internal structure to construct coherent perception from incomplete and noisy signals?

My research focuses on two core questions:

1) When does learning actively shape perception?

2) How does the mind anticipate, revise, and update as new information comes in?

From these foundational questions, I explore perception across modalities, complex environments, and in the face of degraded input, to uncover a common set of principles that govern perceptual inference.

When Does Learning Actually Change What We Perceive?
We constantly accumulate experience, yet much of it remains silent. This work examines when learning actively shapes perception and when it stays latent, using behavioural experiments and EEG to track how memory becomes, or fails to become, behaviourally expressed. Read more
Prediction vs. Revision: How Does the Brain Update Its Interpretations?
Perception unfolds over time. The brain anticipates incoming input and revises interpretations as new evidence arrives. This research investigates how sensory reliability and timing influence these processes and how the brain updates its understanding moment by moment. Read more
Can Vision and Hearing Be Compared in a Common Framework?
If perception reflects shared inferential principles, we need a way to express different senses in the same representational language. This work develops cross-modal feature-space methods that allow auditory and visual precision and bias to be directly compared. Read more
What Can Music Reveal About Perceptual Inference?
Music blends structured expectations with rich acoustic detail. By dissociating bottom-up cues from learned structure, this research explores how sensory evidence and prior knowledge are weighted in complex auditory scenes. Read more
How Does the Brain Compensate When Input Breaks Down?
Sensory systems naturally degrade, and some losses, like hearing, cannot be reversed. Signals are also often noisy or ambiguous. This research examines how prior knowledge, such as a familiar voice, and expectations, such as contextual cues, can be leveraged to improve perception and support effective communication under challenging conditions.
