About

I study how the brain uses prior experience to make sense of the world.

Perception is not passive. What we hear and see is shaped by memory, attention, and the statistical structure of our environments. I am continually fascinated by how the brain integrates information across time and across senses to resolve uncertainty in real time.

My research began in auditory cognition, examining how long-term memory supports listening in complex environments such as speech and music. Over time, this focus has broadened into a more general question: how does stored knowledge interact with incoming sensory information to shape perception across modalities, across individuals, and across the lifespan?

Using behavioural methods and high-temporal-resolution neuroimaging (EEG), I investigate when learned information guides perception, when it remains latent, and how these processes vary across people. I am particularly interested in developing integrative models that connect memory, attention, working memory, and multisensory inference within a shared framework.

At its core, my work seeks to understand how experience allows the brain to adapt—to ambiguity, to change, and to the richness of real-world environments.

Outside the lab, I’m happiest immersed in music, reading widely, and spending time outdoors.
